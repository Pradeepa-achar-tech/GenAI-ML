Hard-coded T fails on different lighting; **Otsu** auto-picks T by maximising between-class variance; **adaptive threshold** picks a different T for each local neighbourhood, making it robust to uneven illumination. Together, filter+threshold is the standard preprocessing pipeline before contour detection, OCR, or any classical segmentation.',

              theory: '**Convolution kernels** — the maths under filtering:\n• A `(3, 3)` kernel of all `1/9` averages each pixel with its 8 neighbours → **box blur** (cheap, blurry).\n• A 2D Gaussian centred at the kernel middle and decaying outwards → **Gaussian blur** (the gold standard for noise reduction; preserves edge orientation).\n• `[[0,-1,0],[-1,5,-1],[0,-1,0]]` → **sharpen** (boosts the centre relative to neighbours).\n• `[[-1,-1,-1],[-1,8,-1],[-1,-1,-1]]` → **edge enhance** (highlights local contrast).\n\nOpenCV gives you ready-made functions:\n• **`cv2.blur(img, ksize)`** — box blur.\n• **`cv2.GaussianBlur(img, ksize, sigmaX)`** — Gaussian; ksize MUST be odd (3, 5, 7…).\n• **`cv2.medianBlur(img, ksize)`** — replaces each pixel with the median of its neighbourhood; eats salt-and-pepper noise without softening edges.\n• **`cv2.bilateralFilter(img, d, sigmaColor, sigmaSpace)`** — edge-preserving smoother; **slow but beautiful** — used in beauty filters, denoising before OCR.\n• **`cv2.filter2D(img, -1, kernel)`** — apply ANY custom kernel.\n\n**Sharpen via unsharp mask**:\n```\nblur = cv2.GaussianBlur(img, (0, 0), 3)\nsharp = cv2.addWeighted(img, 1.5, blur, -0.5, 0)\n```\nThe trick: subtract a blurred copy from the original (with weight 1.5 vs −0.5) and you boost the high-frequency components. This is what every photo editor calls "Sharpen".\n\n**Thresholding**:\n• **`cv2.threshold(src, thresh, maxval, type)`** — returns `(used_thresh, binary_image)`. Types:\n  - `THRESH_BINARY`: pixel > T → maxval, else 0.\n  - `THRESH_BINARY_INV`: inverted version.\n  - `THRESH_TRUNC`: pixel > T → T, else unchanged.\n  - `THRESH_TOZERO` / `_INV`: zero out one side.\n• **`THRESH_OTSU` flag** (added with `+`): ignores your `thresh` arg and computes Otsu T from the histogram. Pass `thresh=0` as a placeholder.\n• **`cv2.adaptiveThreshold(src, maxval, method, type, blockSize, C)`** — picks T per neighbourhood. `method` is `ADAPTIVE_THRESH_MEAN_C` or `ADAPTIVE_THRESH_GAUSSIAN_C`. `blockSize` MUST be odd (11, 21, 31). `C` is a constant subtracted from the mean (try 2-10).\n\n**Morphological operations** — the cleanup step after threshold:\n• **Erosion** (`cv2.erode`) shrinks white regions; kills small specks.\n• **Dilation** (`cv2.dilate`) grows white regions; fills small holes.\n• **Opening** (`MORPH_OPEN`) = erode then dilate; removes specks while keeping shape size.\n• **Closing** (`MORPH_CLOSE`) = dilate then erode; fills holes inside shapes.\n• **Gradient** (`MORPH_GRADIENT`) = dilate − erode; outline of shapes.\n\n**Why blur before threshold**: thresholding amplifies every speck of noise into a separate connected component.
